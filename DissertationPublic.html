On the Integration of Cognitive and Social Pressures in Referring Expression Production: Evidence from the Language of Politics

In this dissertation, I develop a unified model of referring expression production that integrates three pressures known to shape linguistic choice — referential informativity, social-indexical identity construction, and cognitive cost — within a socially-enriched extension of the Rational Speech Act (RSA) framework. Taking sociopolitical identity and ideology as my empirical domain, I present three studies spanning a range of political temperatures that all exhibit variation conditioned by a rational trade-off between social and cognitive pressures. First, a corpus analysis of some 15,000 news articles from Breitbart News, NPR, and PinkNews shows that the referring expressions trans woman, transgender woman, and biological male are deployed in to ideological ends such that neither a purely referential nor a purely cost-based account can explain their distributions. A pair of experiments on gender-neutral role nouns (e.g. congressperson, firefighter) then moves the inquiry to the individual level, showing that production is jointly conditioned by political identity, gender ideology, and lexical frequency, while processing is sensitive only to lexical frequency. Finally, a novel-word-learning study closes the loop by experimentally controlling exposure to terms like crowdcloaking and herdblurring produced by fictional left- or right-leaning users, showing that sociopolitical alignment modulates referring expression production above and beyond the effects of exposure. Together, the three studies argue that referring expression production is best understood as a rational process which simultaneously balances cognitive and social work, and that the architecture for combining them is both empirically necessary and formally tractable.
